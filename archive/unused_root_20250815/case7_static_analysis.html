Use of Artificial Intelligence in Engineering Practice

Case Reference: NSPE BER Case 24-02 | Date: Friday, July 18, 2025
Generated: 2025-07-28

Overview

Engineer A, an experienced environmental engineer, faces ethical decisions about using open-source AI software for creating comprehensive environmental reports and AI-assisted drafting tools for engineering design documents. The case examines the boundaries of ethical AI use in professional engineering practice.

Key Issues: Competence requirements, confidentiality obligations, professional disclosure, and maintaining responsible charge over AI-assisted work.

Context: Client W Engagement

Client W retains Engineer A to prepare a comprehensive report addressing the manufacture, use, and characteristics of an organic compound identified as an emerging contaminant of concern. Engineer A is also tasked with developing engineering design documents (plans and specifications) for modifications to groundwater infrastructure at the same site.

Background: Engineer A is an experienced environmental engineer with PE license and several years of experience, known for strong technical expertise but less confident in technical writing. Has been observing the site for over a year.

üìÖ Loss of Mentor Support

Context: Professional Support Gap

Engineer B, who previously provided guidance and quality assurance reviews to refine Engineer A's report drafts, recently retired and is no longer available in a work capacity.

Impact: Engineer A faces the challenge of delivering professional reports without the review and mentorship from Engineer B that they had previously relied upon.

üìÖ AI Technology Adoption Decision

Context: Seeking Alternative Support

Faced with the need to deliver both the report and engineering design documents without Engineer B's review, Engineer A opts to use open-source artificial intelligence (AI) software to create an initial draft of the necessary report and AI-assisted drafting tools to generate preliminary design documents.

Key Detail: The AI drafting software was new to the market and Engineer A had no previous experience with the tool. The AI software was open-source.

‚öñÔ∏è Decision Point 1: AI-Generated Report Creation

Actual Action Taken: Report Generation Process

Ethical Question: Was Engineer A's use of AI to create the report text ethical, given that Engineer A thoroughly checked the report?

What Actually Happened: Engineer A gathered relevant information provided by Client W and relied on AI software to synthesize the information and generate an initial draft. After a few refining prompts, Engineer A received a first draft from the AI software. Engineer A then conducted a thorough review of the report, cross-checking key facts against professional journal articles, verifying phrasing through search engine queries to ensure no plagiarism, and making minor adjustments to personalize the content. Engineer A did not cite the use of AI software and submitted the draft to Client W with their professional seal.

Ethical Analysis of Options:

Option 1: Ethical AI Use with Proper Safeguards (BER Partial Approval)

BER Finding: "Engineer A's use of AI in report writing was partly ethical, and partly unethical."

‚úÖ Ethical Aspects (What Engineer A Did Right):

  • Maintained competence: Engineer A was qualified as an experienced environmental engineer to analyze groundwater monitoring data and assess contaminant risks [I.2]
  • Thorough review process: Engineer A performed comprehensive review, cross-checked key facts against professional sources, and made adjustments to personalize content [II.2.b]
  • Professional oversight: Final document remained under Engineer A's direction and control, similar to work initially drafted by intern staff [II.2.b]
  • No misrepresentation: Engineer A did not misrepresent qualifications or technical expertise, and AI-generated text contained no inaccuracies [I.5]

‚ùå Unethical Aspects (What Engineer A Failed to Do):

  • Confidentiality violation: Uploading Client W's information into open-source AI interface was "tantamount to placing the Client's private information in the public domain" without client consent [II.1.c]
  • Missing citations: AI-generated report lacked citations of pertinent technical authority documents, failing to give proper credit [III.9]
  • Inadequate documentation: Absent professional level of care, diligence, and documentation required for ethical AI use [III.9]
BER Conclusion: "Ethical use of AI to create the report text must satisfy all pertinent requirements" - Engineer A satisfied competence and review requirements but failed confidentiality and attribution requirements.

Option 2: Fully Ethical AI Implementation (What Should Have Been Done)

Use AI with proper confidentiality protections, client consent, and complete documentation.

Requirements for Ethical AI Use:

  • Client consent: Obtain explicit permission from Client W before sharing any confidential information with AI systems [II.1.c]
  • Proper attribution: Include appropriate citations of technical authority documents and acknowledge AI contributions [III.9]
  • Professional documentation: Maintain professional level of care, diligence, and documentation throughout the AI-assisted process [III.9]
  • Maintain competence: Continue thorough technical review while being qualified in the subject matter [I.2]

Potential Challenges:

  • Client may refuse: Client might not consent to AI use, eliminating this option [II.1.c]
  • Additional complexity: Proper documentation and attribution requirements add administrative burden
  • Evolving standards: Professional standards for AI use are still developing
  • Technology limitations: Open-source AI may not provide adequate confidentiality protections
BER Guidance: AI can be used ethically if all professional requirements are satisfied, including confidentiality, attribution, and proper oversight.
Analysis Summary: This decision involves balancing technological efficiency with professional responsibilities including confidentiality, attribution, and maintaining competence. The key tension is between leveraging AI capabilities and ensuring full professional oversight.

‚öñÔ∏è Decision Point 2: AI-Assisted Design Documents

Actual Action Taken: AI-Generated Design with Inadequate Review

Ethical Question: Was Engineer A's use of AI-assisted drafting tools to create the engineering design documents ethical, given that Engineer A reviewed the design at a high level?

What Actually Happened: Engineer A used AI-assisted drafting tools to generate preliminary design of the plans, including basic layouts and technical specifications. Engineer A completed only a cursory review of the AI-generated plans and adjusted certain elements to align with site-specific conditions. Engineer A did not cite the AI-assisted drafting tools used to generate the engineering design documents.

Ethical Analysis of Options:

Option 1: High-Level Review Only (What Engineer A Did - UNETHICAL)

BER Finding: "The use of AI-assisted drafting tools by Engineer A was not unethical per se. However, Engineer A's misuse of the tool, by failing to maintain Responsible Charge over the AI tool and its output before sealing the document and providing it to Client W, was unethical."

‚ö†Ô∏è Apparent Benefits (Misleading):

  • Engineer competence: Engineer A had "several years of experience" and "strong technical expertise" - not incompetent like BER Case 98-3 [I.2]
  • Efficiency gains: AI-assisted drafting can increase productivity when properly supervised
  • Technology precedent: Similar to accepted CAD/CADD systems established in BER Case 90-6

‚ùå Critical Ethical Failures:

  • Lost responsible charge: Cursory review violated "responsible charge" requirements - Engineer A was "operating in a compromised manner" without proper oversight [II.2.b, III.8.a]
  • Insufficient verification: High-level review was inadequate for comprehensive verification process required for sealed documents [II.2.b]
  • Safety failures discovered: Client W found "misaligned dimensions and omission of key safety features required by local regulations" [I.1, II.2.a]
  • Public welfare compromise: Errors could have led to regulatory noncompliance and safety hazards [I.1]
BER Conclusion: "Engineer A's oversight of engineering plans was inadequate, raising ethical concerns. AI-generated technical work requires at least the same level of scrutiny as human-created work."

Option 2: Proper AI Use with Comprehensive Review (ETHICAL APPROACH)

BER Guidance: "Much like directing an engineering intern to solve a problem, responsible use of AI requires an engineer to outline solution guidelines and constraints... Only after the engineer in Responsible Charge has satisfied themselves that the proposed solution is in accordance with their own and professional standards should the design/report be accepted."

‚úÖ Requirements for Ethical AI Drafting:

  • Maintain responsible charge: Engineer must be "actively engaged in the engineering process, from conception to completion" per NSPE Position Statement 10-1778 [II.2.b, III.8.a]
  • Comprehensive verification: AI-generated technical work requires at least the same level of scrutiny as human-created work [II.2.b]
  • Experienced-based review: Provide quality assurance review, engage in critical discussions, challenge AI recommendations [I.2, II.2.a]
  • Professional understanding: Engineer must understand all proposed solutions before accepting the design [II.2.b]

‚ö†Ô∏è Challenges and Requirements:

  • Cannot replace judgment: "Technology must not replace or be used as a substitute for engineering judgment" per BER Case 98-3
  • Requires expertise: Engineer must have requisite background, education, and training to be proficient with AI tool limitations
  • Time intensive: Proper verification may require significant time investment
  • Professional development: AI "cannot replicate" elements like mentorship and professional development
Key Precedent: BER Case 90-6 established engineers can use computer tools, but "must have requisite background, education and training to be proficient with the dynamics" and tool limitations.
Analysis Summary: This decision centers on maintaining 'responsible charge' over sealed engineering documents. The tension is between efficiency gains from AI drafting and the professional requirement for personal direction and control over work bearing the PE seal.

‚öñÔ∏è Decision Point 3: Disclosure of AI Usage

Context: Professional Transparency Considerations

Ethical Question: If the use of AI was acceptable, did Engineer A have an ethical obligation to disclose the use of AI in any form to the Client?

BER Analysis: The Board examined whether Engineer A had professional or ethical obligations to disclose AI use to Client W, considering both existing precedents and emerging standards for transparency in AI-assisted engineering work.

Ethical Analysis of Options:

Option 1: No Disclosure Required (BER Primary Finding)

BER Conclusion: "Similar to other software used in the design or detailing process, Engineer A has no professional or ethical obligation to disclose AI use to Client W (unless such disclosure is required under Engineer A's contract with Client W)."

‚úÖ BER Reasoning Against Mandatory Disclosure:

  • Software tool precedent: AI treated similar to other design software (CAD, CADD) that don't require disclosure [II.2.b]
  • No universal guidelines: "At the time of the BER's review of this case there is no universal guideline mandating AI disclosure in engineering work"
  • Professional precedent: BER Case 98-3 established no disclosure required for CD-ROM design tools
  • Contract governs: Disclosure only required if specified in Engineer A's contract with Client W

‚ö†Ô∏è Considerations for Voluntary Disclosure:

  • Client observations: Client W noted report "read as if written by two different authors" and found design issues
  • Attribution concerns: Code section III.9 requires giving credit "to those to whom credit is due" [III.9]
  • Trust building: Proactive disclosure could have prevented misunderstandings and strengthened trust
Legal Standard: No professional obligation to disclose unless contractually required, but voluntary disclosure may be beneficial for transparency.

Option 2: Best Practice Transparency (BER Recommendation)

BER Guidance: "Ethical principles favor transparency when AI plays a substantial role in generating work products. To uphold ethical standards, engineers integrating AI into their practice should adopt rigorous verification processes and consider disclosing AI involvement when it plays a significant role in the final product."

‚úÖ Arguments for Voluntary Best Practice:

  • Ethical principles favor transparency: When AI substantially contributes to work products [I.5, III.3]
  • Prevent misunderstandings: Could have addressed Client W's observations about inconsistent writing styles
  • Build trust: Proactive transparency demonstrates professional integrity
  • Acknowledge contributions: AI "fundamentally shaped the report and design documents, warranting disclosure" under Code section III.9 [III.9]

‚ö†Ô∏è Implementation Challenges:

  • Defining "substantial role": No clear guidelines on when AI involvement becomes substantial enough to warrant disclosure
  • Evolving standards: Professional practices for AI disclosure are still developing
  • Client reaction uncertainty: Unknown how clients will respond to AI disclosure
  • Competitive concerns: Other engineers may not disclose, creating competitive disadvantage
BER Recommendation: "Best practices suggest informing clients when AI substantially contributes to a work product" for transparency and trust-building.
Analysis Summary: This decision addresses transparency and disclosure obligations regarding AI tool usage. The key consideration is balancing the absence of explicit disclosure requirements with principles of honesty and the evolving nature of AI technology in professional practice.
